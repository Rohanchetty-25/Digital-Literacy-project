# Case Study: Phishing Attacks Targeting College Students

## What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cybercrime where attackers impersonate legitimate organizations or individuals to trick victims into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, bank details, or personal identification numbers. These attacks typically occur through deceptive emails, text messages, or fake websites designed to look authentic.

## How It Typically Happens

A common phishing attack targeting college students follows this pattern:

1. **The Bait:** The student receives an email appearing to come from the university's IT department, library, or financial aid office. The email often creates urgency, claiming the student's account will be suspended unless they verify their credentials immediately.

2. **The Trap:** The email contains a link that leads to a fake login page that looks identical to the university's official portal. Unsuspecting students enter their username and password.

3. **The Exploitation:** Once the attacker obtains the credentials, they gain access to the student's email, academic records, and potentially financial information. They may use the compromised account to launch further attacks on the student's contacts.

# Who Is Targeted

College students are prime targets because they frequently access institutional systems, often use weak passwords, and may not be well-versed in recognizing sophisticated scams. First-year students are especially vulnerable as they are still learning about campus systems and procedures.

# Consequences

The consequences of falling victim to a phishing attack can be severe:
- Unauthorized access to academic records and personal information
- Financial loss if banking details are compromised
- Identity theft leading to long-term credit damage
- Academic disruption if institutional accounts are locked or misused
- Emotional distress and loss of trust in digital systems

In one documented case at a major Indian university, a single phishing email compromised over 200 student accounts within 48 hours, leading to widespread academic disruption and requiring weeks of IT intervention to resolve.
